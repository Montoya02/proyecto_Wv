Minimalist Money: Personal Finance for People Who Hate Finance

Published: May 28, 2025 Reading Time: 8 min

I get it, the moment you hear terms like “budgeting spreadsheets” or “asset allocation,” it’s easy to feel overwhelmed. You’re definitely not alone. Many prefer watching a show over tackling financial planning. The great news is, managing your money can be straightforward and efficient.

There are many different profiles for people when it comes to money, so here you will read different approaches and strategies that you can apply depending on your personality. The brightside is that you can even mix some of them and create some plans with them.

The 80/20 Rule for Your Wallet

Have you heard about the 80/20 rule? Basically, 80% of your financial success comes from 20% of what you actually do. So why stress over every latte? Focus on the big moves that make a real difference.

Here’s your shortlist:

  • Where your paycheck goes each month
  • How much you're saving (and where)
  • The debt you owe

That's it. Master these three things, and you're already ahead of most people.

I used to use apps to track every expense, but that didn't change that much for me, usually is just extra stress you put to yourself. So the thing that we really need it's to have a clear idea and split of our money in these categories so we can plan what to do next (here is when investment often appears)

The Two-Account Trick

Forget complicated budgets with 15 categories. Use just two accounts:

account_balance

Account 1: Bills & Necessities

This covers rent, utilities, groceries, minimum debt payments – all the stuff you can't avoid.

shopping_cart

Account 2: Everything Else

Fun money, dining out, shopping, hobbies. When this account hits zero, you're done spending until next month.

It's budgeting without the spreadsheet headache.

#!#!#!#Emergency Fund: Start Stupidly Small

Everyone says you need 3-6 months of expenses saved. That's great advice, but if you're starting from zero, it's also paralyzing. Instead, start with $500. Just $500.

That small buffer handles most of life's minor emergencies – car repairs, medical co-pays, or that time your laptop decides to die during finals week. Once you hit $500, bump it to $1,000. Then $2,000. Baby steps work.

Investing: Keep It Brain-Dead Simple

Forget trying to outsmart the market. You don’t need to pick stocks or watch CNBC all day (unless you want to). Just set it up and let your money grow.

An example of the lazy investor's portfolio:

  • 70% total stock market index fund
  • 30% bond index fund

That's it. Rebalance once a year. This boring approach beats most active traders and requires about 30 minutes of your time annually.

Imagine this: you invest $300 a month for 35 years with a 10% annual return. You’d end up with over $1 million. If you just saved that money in a regular bank account, you’d have about $126,000. That’s the power of compound interest (we’ll break it down in another article) — it can make you rich without being a financial genius.

Debt: The Snowball vs. Avalanche Debate

There are two main ways to pay off debt: Avalanche: Pay off the debt with the highest interest rate first. This saves you more money over time. Snowball: Pay off the smallest debt first. This gives you quick wins and a motivation boost. Which one’s better? Honestly, whichever one keeps you going. If wiping out a balance makes you feel like a boss, go snowball. If you love seeing the numbers work in your favor, avalanche is for you. The real win? Sticking with it. Doesn’t matter how you attack your debt — as long as you’re moving forward, you’re doing it right.

Here's the truth: The best method is the one you'll actually stick with. If seeing debts disappear motivates you, go snowball. If saving money on interest fires you up, go avalanche. Both work if you work them.

The 24-Hour Rule for Big Purchases

Before buying something that feels like an upgrade — whether it’s a new gadget, designer item, or spontaneous indulgence — give yourself a day to think. For bigger splurges, wait a few more.

You might realize that what seemed essential in the moment doesn’t really hold up the next day.

It’s not about guilt — it’s about making sure your purchases truly align with your values, not just a passing impulse.

Track Your Net Worth, Not Your Daily Spending

Forget about logging every transaction. Instead, check your net worth monthly. Add up everything you own, subtract what you owe. That's your financial scorecard.

Use a simple app or even a basic spreadsheet. The goal is watching that number grow over time, even if it's slow at first.

The "Good Enough" Philosophy

Your financial plan doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be consistent. A mediocre plan you follow beats a perfect plan you abandon after two weeks.

Don't let perfect be the enemy of good. Start where you are, with what you have.

Red Flags That Actually Matter

While we're keeping things simple, watch out for these warning signs:

  • Using credit cards for groceries because checking is empty
  • Borrowing money for vacations
  • Not knowing roughly how much you spend each month
  • Having zero savings while making decent money

If any of these sound familiar, it's time to get serious (but still keep it simple).

Personal finance isn't about restriction – it's about freedom. The freedom to not stress about money. The freedom to take risks. The freedom to be generous. The freedom to sleep well at night.

You don't need to become a finance expert. You just need to master the basics, automate what you can, and stay consistent. The rest will take care of itself.

Invest in Yourself (It's Not Mandatory, But It Pays Off)

Here's something most finance articles won't tell you: sometimes the best investment isn't in the stock market – it's in yourself. You don't have to do this, but learning new skills or deepening your knowledge can literally pay dividends.

Why investing in yourself works:

  • A single book might teach you something that saves or makes you thousands
  • Online courses can help you switch careers or get promoted
  • Learning about side hustles could create extra income streams
  • Understanding taxes better might save you money every year
school

Smart ways to invest in your education:

  • Read one personal finance book per month (libraries are free, but having your own copy means you can highlight and reference it)
  • Take online courses in skills relevant to your career
  • Listen to podcasts during commutes instead of music sometimes
  • Follow financial educators on YouTube or blogs

The ROI on education can be massive. Spending $20 on a book that teaches you to negotiate your salary better could net you thousands. A $200 course that helps you start a side business might pay for itself in the first month.

Start small: Pick one area where you want to improve – whether it's understanding investing, learning a new skill for work, or figuring out how to start that side project you've been thinking about. Then find one resource (book, course, or mentor) to help you get there.

Remember, you're not just managing money – you're building the skills to make more of it.

The Bottom Line

Start with just one thing from this article. One small step — whether it’s setting up automatic savings or finally checking your net worth. These small actions add up over time.

You can also mix and match some of these ideas—for example, combining the two-account strategy with automation. The key is to find what fits your personality and what you’ll actually stick with.

Managing your money doesn’t have to be complicated. Keep it simple, stay consistent, and keep making progress.
